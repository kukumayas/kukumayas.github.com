语言网络，结构、功能和进化

 2015-01-23 by Z. Jiang

All languages share some universal tendencies at different levels of organization: the phoneme inventories, the syntactic and semantic categories and structures, as well as the conceptualisations being expressed. There are also universal statistical trends in human languages, such as Zipf’s law.

Language is a complex network

Language is clearly an example of a complex dynamical system. In this context, two main features seem to be shared by most complex networks. The first is their small world structure. In spite of their large size and sparseness these webs are very well connected: it is very easy to reach a given element from another one through a small number of jumps (e.g. six degrees of separation, six jumps). The second these webs are extremely heterogeneous: most elements are connected to one or two other elements and only a handful of them have a very large number of links. These hubs are the key components of web complexity, their loss or failure has very negative con- sequences for system performance, sometimes even pro- moting a system’s collapse.

Three kinds of language network

Co-occurrence networks

Two words are linked if they appear to- gether within at least one sentence. We can consider the order in which words co-occur and repre- sent that in directed graphs; hubs for words with low semantic content but important grammatical functions.Content words such as mama or juice are important at early stages but fade out in later stages as function words (particu- larly you, the, a) emerge together with grammar.

Syntactic networks

Dependency grammar is a useful linguistic framework to build up syntactic networks. Here hubs are functional words but we can see that the in- and out- degrees are different from the previous web based on precendence relations.
